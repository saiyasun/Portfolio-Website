**At first, the contest intimidated me more than I wanted to admit.**

Building an entire game felt too big.

Too open.

Too easy to fail at.

So I did what I usually do when I am nervous about starting.

I researched.

For about a day, I looked into game jams, tools, examples, workflows, and anything else that made me feel like I was preparing.

Some of it was useful.

But at a certain point, I had to admit what was happening.

I was procrastinating.

### What I Started Learning

Once I actually started, I realized how much I did not know.

Game design was its own world.

Planning mattered more than I expected.

Animation was harder than it looked.

Even simple things required decisions I had never really thought about before.

I was not just learning how to make a game. I was learning how many small pieces have to work together for a game to feel like anything at all.

That was overwhelming.

But it was also exciting.

### Seeing What AI Could Do

The most surprising part was seeing how much AI could do.

One prompt could generate a whole webpage in less than five minutes.

That is still kind of insane to me.

It made me wonder if learning to code was going to become obsolete.

And if I am being honest, there was a small fear underneath that.

Not dramatic.

But real.

The fear of being replaced.

I have spent a lot of time trying to learn programming seriously. So seeing a tool produce things that would have taken me hours, or maybe days, felt strange.

Impressive, but strange.

### Where AI Started Breaking Down

Then I used it more.

And the limitations became obvious.

AI is powerful, but at least right now, it is not replacing everyone.

It still struggles with things people care about a lot:

- images
- video
- animation
- consistency
- small details that affect how something feels

It usually does better with logic-based tasks.

But even then, it misunderstands things more often than people might expect.

You can type what you want in plain English, but creating something decent still requires technical knowledge.

English is not magic.

### The Tiger Problem

One issue made that clear.

I had a tiger character with an attack that the game was not recognizing.

I kept asking AI to fix it.

It kept giving me answers.

But the problem did not actually get fixed.

Eventually, I had to debug it myself.

Because I had enough basic JavaScript knowledge, I could look through the logic and understand what was going wrong.

If I did not have that, I might have been stuck there for a long time.

That was an important moment.

AI could help me move faster.

But it could not replace understanding.

### Learning to Direct

The biggest shift was not that I learned some new syntax.

It was that I learned how to direct better.

AI gives better results when the task is broken down into small, clear steps.

Not vague instructions.

Not one giant request.

Small directions.

Specific constraints.

Clear examples.

That forced me to break down my own thinking. If I could not explain what I wanted clearly, I probably did not understand it clearly enough yet.

That is a different skill from just writing code.

But it is still a skill.

### What Actually Matters

This experience changed how I think about the fear of being replaced.

If someone's only skill is knowing Python syntax, then yes, AI is probably a threat.

But I do not think the real skill is memorizing syntax.

The real skill is:

- understanding the problem
- breaking it into smaller pieces
- finding a path toward a solution
- communicating clearly
- knowing enough to judge whether the output is actually good

That is the part that still matters.

Maybe it matters even more now.

### Is It Cheating?

At first, part of me felt like using AI was cheating.

But the more I thought about it, the less that made sense.

If using AI is cheating, then where does that line stop?

Is using a high-level language cheating because I am not writing assembly?

Is using a framework cheating because I am not building everything from scratch?

Is using a search engine cheating because I did not memorize every answer?

Technology keeps moving the baseline.

That does not mean effort disappears.

It means the work changes.

### Where I Landed

I do not think AI is the enemy.

I think it is a tool.

A powerful one.

And like every major tool, it changes what people need to learn.

We went from horses to cars.

That did not mean movement stopped mattering.

It meant people had to adapt to a new way of moving.

I think AI is similar.

It is not just about being replaced.

It is about learning how to work differently.

That does not remove the fear completely.

But it gives me a better question to focus on.

Not:

> Will AI replace me?

But:

> How do I become the kind of person who can use it well?
