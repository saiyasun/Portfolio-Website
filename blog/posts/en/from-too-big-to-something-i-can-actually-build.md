**I thought I needed to build something big.**

Something original.

Something deep.

Something that felt like a real game.

So that is where my mind went.

### Where It Didn't Add Up

Before I started building anything, I looked at the current submissions for Vibe Jam.

Then I went to the official site and started playing some of them: [vibej.am](https://vibej.am/).

That is when things started to feel different.

Most of the games were simple.

Not bad simple. Just focused.

They did not have grand stories, mind-blowing graphics, or massive systems. They loaded quickly. I could usually tell what I was supposed to do within a few seconds. And they ran smoothly on my laptop, which is not exactly powerful.

That forced me to look at my own idea more honestly.

I was trying to make something impressive before I had made something playable.

### The Shift

That was the first real shift.

My idea did not need to be revolutionary.

It did not need to prove everything I could imagine.

It just needed to work.

Something simple, clear, and playable would already be enough.

### Breaking My Own Thinking

Instead of opening a game engine and immediately trying to build, I did something else first.

I ran my ideas through ChatGPT.

Not to generate the game for me.

I wanted help simplifying my thinking.

At the time, I had this massive idea in my head. It was the kind of thing that only makes sense when it is still imaginary. A full Final Fantasy-style game with multiple characters, different countries, investigation segments, combat systems, story arcs, and a final boss where everything connects.

In my head, it pulled from *Final Fantasy*, *Kingdom Hearts*, and *The World Ends With You*.

Which is cool as an idea.

But completely unrealistic for a game jam.

So I asked ChatGPT a simpler question:

> What can I extract from this?

### From Too Big to Usable

It gave me a list of smaller ideas:

- **Echo Snap** - react to audio cues
- **Can You Hear It?** - identify sounds buried in noise
- **Tone Sprint** - distinguish similar sounds quickly
- **One Strike** - attack only at the perfect moment
- **Fake Out** - enemies telegraph attacks, but sometimes lie
- **Don't Fight** - avoid escalating conflict
- **Voices in Paradise** - a peaceful world with hidden manipulation
- **One Day** - live a single day through choices
- **As One** - control one of three characters randomly in combat

For the first time, the project felt smaller without feeling empty.

One idea stood out.

### Choosing Something I Could Actually Make

**As One.**

Not because it was the best idea.

Because it felt like something I could actually build.

And at this point, that mattered more.

I still wanted the game to have some emotional weight. I did not want it to feel like a random prototype with no context. So I kept a loose backstory in my head.

Nothing fully written out.

Just enough to guide decisions.

The idea came from the ending of that bigger game I knew I could not make. Instead of trying to build the whole thing, I took one piece from it and turned that into the game.

### The Structure

The rough version was simple.

The opening screen would show a city in turmoil.

Then the player would be dropped straight into battle.

You control one of three characters:

- a police officer
- a child with a dog in an attack stance
- a man in a broken environment

Each character changes the background:

- police officer - city
- child - paradise-like world
- man - abandoned house

Each round, you fight one of a few enemy types. Each character has three actions. The characters you are not controlling can act as assists.

The round ends when you win or die.

The game ends when all characters die, all enemies are defeated, or time runs out.

That was enough structure to start.

### Letting It Stay Incomplete

There were still ideas I wanted to explore.

Like an emotion system for the child.

Or having the environments affect gameplay in different ways.

But I knew if I tried to solve everything at once, I would probably never start.

So I let it stay incomplete.

That felt uncomfortable, but useful.

### Learning How to Start

At that point, I realized I did not need more ideas.

I needed to build.

So I watched a video on how to vibe code a game: [How to Vibe Code a Game](https://youtu.be/yKyjcbQiar4?si=RQpjazyGVzT-GTeg).

I was not trying to follow it exactly. I just wanted to understand how to get something on the screen.

Then I started gathering assets, testing things, and trying to make the project real in the smallest way possible.

Nothing polished.

Just something real.

### Where This Goes

I do not know if this game will be good.

I do not know if I will finish it.

But I do know I made an important shift.

I went from trying to build something big to building something I could actually start.

And right now, that is enough.
