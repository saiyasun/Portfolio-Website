# Learning Chinese in Slow Motion

Core engaged. Bar loaded. Breathing getting heavier. 

My arms shake as every muscle fiber fires to move the weight. The outside world fades as all my focus goes into finishing this rep. 

As I clear the final few inches, I can only hear: 

A Mandarin podcast, slowed to a crawl, playing through my earbuds.

That was my reality for about a month.

I experimented with listening to native Mandarin Chinese content at 0.25x speed. 

No subtitles. No pausing. No repeating. Just letting the sound wash over me as I went about my day.

I got this idea through an email from Idahosa Ness, the founder of a program called *The Mimic Method*. In it, he shared the story of a woman who used this method to essentially "slow down" Portuguese speech. 

Up until that point, I had been living here in Taiwan for nearly 1<sup>1/2</sup> years. Even though I was surrounded by Mandarin daily, I struggled to understand naturally spoken Chinese. The experience shared in that email mirrored my exact problem but the method was something that never crossed my mind to try.

I tried so many other things with limited success so figured that it couldn't hurt to test it out.

Starting from the morning after the email, I began listening almost exclusively to content designed for native Mandarin speakers.

No over enunciated speech. No "baby talk". Just native-level language and prosody.

I listened when I wasn't in class, studying, meeting friends, or doing anything that required sustained focus. I primarily used YouTube to take advantage of the ability to slow down videos and listen in the background.

I did this for next 28 days with vsrying levels of intensity — making sure to document my findings. 

In the beginning, I averaged around 5-6 hours of total listening time. However, the average decreased to around 1-2 hours towards the end as I was dealing with exams and general listening fatigue.

When the experiment was over, I didn't find any marked improvement in my listening ability.

Even now, I still struggle to understand native speakers speaking at their natural speeds in some circumstances. I can't say for sure what effect my experiment had. 

There were too many variables. 

But, this is what I found.

Even without clear improvement, I still walked away with valuable insights.

Because what I found points to something deeper, not only about Chinese, but about how we perceive language.
