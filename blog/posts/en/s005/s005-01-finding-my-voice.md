I once heard communication expert and YouTuber Vinh Giang describe the voice as an instrument.

But for most of my life, I treated mine more like a blunt tool.

I used it to talk, ask questions, make jokes, and occasionally attempt to sing if the situation called for it, which was thankfully not often. Sometimes I would get a comment on it, positive or negative, but I never thought of my voice as something to train or study. At least not seriously. It was just my voice. It was just how I sounded.

But over the last year and a half, my perspective has shifted.

The way I sound affects how clearly I communicate, how confident I seem, how comfortable I feel speaking in front of people, and even how well I can learn another language. My voice is not just sound coming out of my mouth. It is part of how I present myself to the world.

## More Than a Tool

An instrument is not just something that makes noise. It is designed to make sound with intention. It can be tuned. It can be practiced. Most importantly, it can be improved.

Two people can say the exact same sentence and make it feel completely different. One person can sound nervous. The other can sound confident. One can sound warm. The other can sound bored. The words matter, but the way those words are carried matters too.

It's technical, but also personal.

## What Feels Missing

Whether my voice sounds good or bad, I will leave that up to whoever hears me.

But I do think it is underdeveloped.

Sometimes I speak too quickly. I often speak too quietly. Sometimes my voice feels trapped in my throat instead of resonating. Sometimes I don't sound as clear or expressive as I want to.

And when it comes to singing, I'm completely ignorant.

All I know is that singing is different from talking. I don't know how to go through scales. I can't for the life of me explain the difference between a scale, a key, and a chord. Breath support is still a foreign concept to me.

I want a voice that can not only convey information, but express it.

## Why It Matters

My voice is part of almost everything I want to do.

I use it in presentations, conversations, videos, interviews, friendships, language learning, and everyday life. If I want to explain ideas better, connect with people better, and share my work more confidently, I can't only think about the words I choose. I also have to think about how those words sound when they leave my mouth.

It matters for Mandarin too.

Speaking another language is not just about vocabulary and grammar. It is also about rhythm, tone, mouth shape, intonation, and learning how the language feels physically. If I want to sound more natural in Mandarin, then I need better awareness of my voice in general.

And, anecdotally, I notice that people who sing well often seem to have better control over sound, rhythm, and pronunciation when learning languages. It's not always true. I have met people with great singing voices who still struggle with the basics in their target language. But in my limited experience, singing seems connected to the same kind of awareness that helps with language learning.

If that's true, I think it's worth the effort to get my singing ability to at least a basic level.

So this is not separate from everything else I am working on.

It connects to all of it.

## How I Want To Practice

This series will document my attempt to improve both my speaking and singing voice.

I want to work on:

- breathing
- posture
- resonance
- vocal placement
- articulation
- speaking clarity
- singing basics
- confidence while speaking

Some of this will probably feel awkward. Voice work is personal in a way that makes it easy to cringe at yourself. Recording myself, listening back, noticing bad habits, and trying again can be uncomfortable.

But if weightlifting has taught me anything, it's that the moments when you're most uncomfortable are often where the most progress happens.

I want to treat my voice like a skill, not a fixed trait.

## Goal

I don't expect to sound like a professional singer, voice actor, or motivational speaker in three months. I actually don't know how long it'll take me to noticeably improve.

I'm making this a long-term project for myself and seeing how far I can get in a year, with periodic check-ins along the way.

The goal is to sound more like myself, but clearer.

I want to find the voice that is already there and learn how to use it better.
